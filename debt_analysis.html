Determinants of Debt Among Young Adults in the UK

1. Introduction

This analysis examines what factors affect the debt of 25-year-olds using data from the Next Steps study, an England-based longitudinal project tracking approximately 16,000 young people across eight waves from 2004. During Wave 1, the participants were aged 13/14, and age 25 during Wave 8. The study documented their education, employment, family, and finances. This project employs multiple linear regression to identify different factors across the years that have impacted the debt in Wave 8. Understanding the factors that affect debt could contribute to improving financial education policy for young adults.

2. Data and Methods

2.1 Data Loading and Cleaning

The original dataset contains 5,792 observations. After removing missing and negative values for the debt variable (W8QDEB2 amount in ), the sample size was 2,878. The debt distribution is heavily right-skewed, with most respondents reporting lower levels of debt while only a handful of people have large amounts.

2.2 Variable Selection

I chose 7 independent variables for my model:

  • W8DMARSTAT (marital status): influences financial decisions
  • W8DACTIVITYC (current activity): affects the likelihood of taking on debt and ability to service debt
  • W8CMSEX (gender): analyses gender differences in debt behaviour
  • W8TENURE (housing tenure): represents major financial commitments
  • W1nssecfam (NS-SEC classification of family, Wave 1): captures patterns of advantage/disadvantage
  • W1ethgrpYP (ethnic group, Wave 1): explores cultural differences
  • W6DebtattYP (attitude towards debt, Wave 6): examines whether earlier attitudes predict later behaviour

All variables except W6DebtattYP have nominal values and were factored and relevelled after filtering values 0.

4. Regression Results

4.1 Original Model

The original regression model was statistically significant (F(27, 2585) = 6.85, p < 0.001) but explains only 5.7% of variance (adjusted R = 0.057). Gender is significant (p = 0.006), with females having less debt than males. Housing tenure is the strongest predictor (p < 0.001), implying that those with mortgages have approximately 14,568 more debt on average than those who outright own their houses. Several NS-SEC class values are also significant, suggesting that children raised in lower occupation households grow to have less debt than their counterparts raised in higher managerial occupation households. Furthermore, current activity and attitude towards debt are significant predictors, revealing that students/trainees and those with more positive attitudes towards debt have higher debt.

4.2 Log-Transformed Model

The log-transformed model is also statistically significant (F(27, 2585) = 5.15, p < 0.001) but explains a lesser 4.1% of variance. Housing tenure is the most significant predictor, revealing that on average those with mortgages have 182% more debt than other homeowners. Attitude towards debt also shows significant results debt increases with each 1-unit increase in debt attitude. Married adults and those whose families come from small employer classes also appear to have a higher percentage of debt. Current activity and ethnicity significantly predict debt, with the unemployed and sick/disabled having less debt, and ethnically Pakistani people having 58% less debt than ethnically white people.

reg_data$log_debt <- log(reg_data$W8QDEB2 + 1)

6. Interpretation

Debt among 25-year-olds is overwhelmingly affected by housing tenure. Young homeowners with mortgages carry significantly more debt than those who outright own their homes. This makes sense because at age 25, they are probably at the beginning of their mortgage term, leading to other forms of debt like credit cards or other types of loans.

Current activity also matters, exactly as expected. Results show that students and trainees have higher debts most likely from student loans while the unemployed and those unable to work have lesser debt, not from preference, but because lenders restrict credit without a stable income.

The gender finding females carrying less debt may reflect the 2015 social context where men were more likely to pursue homeownership independently due to traditional gender roles, though research shows this pattern has shifted in recent years with women having more debt than men.

Family socioeconomic classification is an important and interesting predictor of debt. Children from small employer families (typically parents running businesses) carry 45% more debt, likely reflecting entrepreneurial culture where debt is viewed as a business tool, combined with middle-class aspirations requiring borrowing without full parental wealth to avoid loans. On the other hand, children of lower occupational classes like lower managerial/supervisory and routine/semi-routine occupations have lower debts. This could be because lower family income makes it harder to save for deposits, and banks are less willing to lend money without family guarantors. They are also unlikely to have mortgage debt as they probably rent or live with family/friends.

Culture and marital status also affect debt. Ethnically Pakistani respondents have lesser debt, possibly due to South Asian culture of having greater financial support from family or even different religious principles. Marriage increases debt by 32%, likely because couples take on joint financial commitments like mortgages.

7. Limitations

Both models explain minimal variance in debt (adjusted R of 46%). Many factors like education, income, and specific loan types could better explain debt but are unmeasured. Additionally, missing data reduces the accuracy and reliability of the model. The model also does not explain causal relationships between debt and other variables. For example, we cannot determine whether employment causes debt or vice versa.

8. Conclusion

Debt among 25-year-olds in the Next Steps study is mainly affected by housing tenure and varies with current activity and their familys socio-economic classifications. Respondents who bought homes with mortgages or loans have significantly higher debt than those who outright own homes. Students have higher debts likely due to student loans, while the unemployed have lesser debts because they dont have access to loans without a stable source of income. The low explanatory power suggests debt is influenced by many unmeasured individual and contextual factors.
